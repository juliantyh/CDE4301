1. Introduction

This project is done in collaboration with Duke-NUS and SGH to increase the adoption of endoscopic resection by Gastroenterologists for pre-cancerous gastric subepithelial/submucosal lesions (Gastrointestinal Stromal Tumors and Neuroendocrine Neoplasms) and early gastric cancer less than 3 cm, from 2% to 40% within 5 years, by achieving efficacy (R0 resection) and safety (complication rates) comparable to the gold standard of surgical resection and making the procedure easier, safer and faster.

2. Background

2.1. Subepithelial lesions

Subepithelial lesions (SELs) in the digestive tract refer to abnormal lesions originating from the various layers under the mucosa, and upper gastrointestinal SELs are the most common (Su et al., 2021).

They are commonly identified during routine endoscopic procedures. Gastric Subepithelial lesions are a rather frequent finding, occurring in about 0.36% (or around 1 in 300) of routine upper GI-endoscopies (Papanikolaou et al., 2011).

Subepithelial lesions are often incidentally discovered and often go unnoticed by patients until they are discovered during screening for unrelated conditions. If symptoms do exist, chronic anaemia from intermittent GI bleeding is most common (Humphris & Jones, 2008). Other symptoms include abdominal pain and luminal obstruction, depending on the site and size of the lesion. Subepithelial lesions have a 15% risk of malignant potential (Polkowski, 2005). Men and women are equally affected, and most patients are more than 50 years old at the time of diagnosis. (Menon & Buscaglia, 2013)

2.2. Current Standard of Care

Endoscopic Submucosal Dissection (ESD) and Endoscopic Full Thickness Resection (EFTR) are the current standard of care for the resection of subepithelial lesions. They are minimally invasive procedures to remove lesions located beneath the mucosal layer of the digestive tract. They are types of therapeutic endoscopy used to remove large polyps or early cancers without the need for open surgery. The procedures use an endoscope to accurately remove growth without removing large portions of the organ involved.

1) ESD is the current standard-of-care. It is a resection technique where the endoscopist cuts through the mucosa using an electrosurgical knife, locates the lesion and removes it and closes the mucosal defect.

2) EFTR, is a full-thickness or deep wall resection of a small piece of the complete GI wall. It is mainly done using the OVESCO FTRD® System, which is an integrated closure and resection device for use in the lower intestinal tract (Aslanian et al., 2019). It is a resection technique where the endoscopist grabs hold of the lesion, pulls multiple layers of the stomach wall into the cap, clips below the tissue to trap the lesion, then slices through the stomach wall. (Cai et al., 2018)


3. Significance

3.1 Benefits of Endoscopic Resection (ER)

We chose to focus on ER as it has a high efficacy for the resection of GISTs. In the past, patients with the diagnosis of GISTs were mainly treated by open surgery or laparoscopic wedge resection (Madhavan et al., 2018; Ishikawa et al., 2006; Guo et al., 2019), Compared with open surgery, endoscopic therapy has great advantages in surgical time, intraoperative blood loss, and postoperative recovery (Ishikawa et al., 2006; Joensuu et al., 2013; Guo et al., 2019)

Compared to laparoscopic wedge resection, endoscopic treatment provides a clearer operative view to identify a precise resection area without needless extensive excision. Additionally, ER can preserve most structures of the stomach with normal digestive physiology maintained, and patients can get a better quality of life (Meng et al., 2015; Yu et al., 2017; Guo et al., 2019)

3.2 Increasing Demand for Endoscopic Resection (ER)

There is an increasing demand for gastrointestinal endoscopic procedures due to:

  1. An aging population. In 2010, about 1 in 10 Singaporeans were aged 65 and above. A decade later, in 2020, it has risen to about 1 in 6. By 2030, another 10 years later, it would be almost 1 in 4 Singaporeans over 65 (Lee, 2023).
  2. There is also a rising prevalence of gastrointestinal disorders and other chronic conditions around the world. According to the World Health Organization, Stomach Cancer is the 3rd deadliest cancer in the world. In Singapore, it is the 4th most common cause of cancer death in men, and 5th most common in women, claiming 300 to 500 lives every year. The main reason for this is late detection - 2/3 of Stomach Cancer patients are only diagnosed at an advanced stage (Cancer Information - Stomach Cancer (Gastric Cancer) 2022).

The global endoscopic device market size is projected to increase from USD 56.5 billion in 2024 to approximately USD 104.74 billion by 2033. The market is expected to grow at a solid Compound Annual Growth Rate (CAGR) of 7.1% during the forecast period from 2024 to 2033.

Therefore, improving the efficiency and safety of gastrointestinal lesion resection in the endoscopic device industry will save many lives as it is a growing market with a growing demographic that need these procedures.

4.2 Limitations of EFTR

After our clinical immersion, we decided to focus on improving Endoscopic Full Thickness Resection (EFTR) due to the technical challenges faced by SGH Endoscopists that we interviewed when they use the FTRD to do this procedure. This includes problems with visibility, securing the lesion, insufficient wound closure, ease of use and team coordination (Details in Table 1).

The key problems are:

  1. During EFTR, it is difficult to determine if the lesion has been completely removed. Two mistakes can occur. First, the device captures normal tissue instead of the lesion. Second, the lesion is only partially resected by the device.
  2. During EFTR, it is difficult to ensure that the endoscopic clips close mucosal defects sufficiently to prevent perforation of stomach wall and post-procedural bleeding.

Addressing these limitations enables more efficient resections, resulting in better patient outcomes. Due to these technical challenges, FTRD has a limited uptake, leaving ESD as the primary choice despite its longer procedure time and high difficulty. This was validated during our clinical immersion. From our interviews at the SGH Ambulatory Endoscopy Centre (AEC), within the department, only two endoscopists are confident to attempt the FTRD procedure with a high success rate. Additionally, from our interview with an endoscopist based in the USA, only 2% of Gastroenterologists in the USA perform endoscopic resection of subepithelial lesions using the Full Thickness Resection Device, or FTRD®.

6. Value Proposition

To fill the identified gap in the market, we have developed the following value proposition:

Our proposed solution makes the process of endoscopic resection of gastric subepithelial lesions and early gastric cancer just as safe and effective as surgical wedge resection, while being faster than the current standard-of-care endoscopic resection technique, Endoscopic Submucosal Dissection (ESD). It also securely captures the subepithelial lesion and ensures complete resection while reducing the risk of perforation and retaining resection efficiency, which improves on challenges faced by endoscopists when using the FTRD® System.

7.4 Concept Generation

We used the Ideabox method where we specified our challenge, selected the required functions, listed variations and tried different combinations to generate different concepts. By combining different ideas to achieve the desired functions, we generated the following concepts.

Concept 1A and 1B both employ a biomaterial to detect the lesion's position, along with a metal mesh to constrict the lesion. However, Concept 1A incorporates an electrical pressure sensor for lesion detection, while Concept 1B utilizes microneedles and biochemical detection to visually indicate the lesion’s position on the mucosal surface. Both concepts then utilize an Endoloop with clips for wound closure, with the biomaterial also assisting in this process.

Concept 1C streamlines the approaches of Concepts 1A and 1B by utilizing a miniaturized version of the existing Apollo Overstitch technology. This concept creates a purse-string stitch around the lesion, pulling the corners of any flat lesion into a pedunculated shape, facilitating easier cutting.

Concept 2 introduces a "Cookie Cutter" mechanism for full-thickness resection. It features a Micro Ultrasound Probe positioned adjacent to the lesion to monitor potential cuts into vital structures and to ensure complete capture of the lesion. This concept employs a tissue helix/forceps to grasp the lesion, with it acting as a guide to the lesion. The Cookie Cutter is positioned over the lesion and pushes down on the stomach wall for a vacuum seal to be created while suction is applied. These steps prevent the lesion from moving laterally. The Cookie Cutter slices downward around the lesion, resulting in a cylindrical slice of stomach wall. The endoscopist then checks the Ultrasound Probe to ensure that the lesion is completely captured. After confirming that the lesion is secure, the clip is then deployed to trap the lesion and pre-close the wound. Following this, a circular knife—like a camera shutter—or a snare, slices the base of the cylindrical section to complete the full-thickness resection.

7.5 Concept Selection

We evaluated the 4 concepts based on the Must-Have and Nice-To-Have Criteria

We used a Pugh Matrix where we listed our evaluation criteria to evaluate each solution and weighed them based on whether each concept satisfied each criterion.

The Pugh matrix helped us to determine using an unbiased analysis that although Concept 1A and 1B gives the best outcome if successfully developed, Concept 1C and Concept 2 have less negatives with a similar effectiveness. After consulting our clinical mentor, Dr. Ravi and our industry mentor, Mr Peter Cheng, we decided that Concept 2 was the most feasible to design and test. Therefore, we chose to move forward with Concept 2.

8. Design Specifications

9. Concept Design

9.1 Detailed Functional Analysis

Based on the selected Concept 2, we broke down our functions to the key components of the system.

The selected concept consists of 6 overall stages: 1) Place Mini-Ultrasound Probe, 2) Position Cookie Cutter to Capture and Secure Lesion, 3) Downward Slice, 4) Feedback on Complete Capture, 5) Deploy Clip, 6) Slice Base. This prevents the lesion from moving away from the target area, increases procedure visibility and a more uniform and efficient method of completing full thickness resection.

9.2 Detailed Concept Design

Stage 1: Place Mini-Ultrasound Probe

This stage anchors the Miniature Ultrasound Probe beside the lesion to monitor and obtain visual feedback that the lesion is completely captured within the device.

It can perform Dual-Plane Reconstruction (DPR) which makes it possible to display radial and linear ultrasound images simultaneously on a single monitor in real time. By generating two dimensions that correlate directly with one another, DPR allows the user to observe both the penetration and longitudinal border of a region of interest. This makes it possible to more accurately assess tissue invasion and more specifically whether the subepithelial lesion has been captured.

It also has a wide 40 mm Linear Scanning Stroke, allowing an entire lesion to be scanned in one pass, which shortens procedure time. It also has an outer diameter of only 2.5mm, so it is slim enough to pass through 2.8mm scope channels. As a result, they can be used with existing routine endoscopes.

Stage 2: Position Cookie Cutter to Capture and Secure Lesion

This stage is where the endoscope approaches the subepithelial lesion, and the tissue helix descends to grab it. Using tissue helix as guide, the cookie cutter is positioned over the lesion and pushes down on the stomach wall for a vacuum seal to be created while suction is applied.

Stage 3: Downward Slice

This stage is where the cookie cutter slices downward around the subepithelial lesion, creating a cylindrical slice of stomach tissue.

Stage 4: Feedback on Complete Capture

This stage is where the endoscopist checks the ultrasound view from the miniature probe to obtain visual feedback that the lesion is completely captured within the device. Studies show that AI models are available to predict a lesion’s resectability using the tomographic image of the gastrointestinal wall. AI could also be used to obtain confirmation that the lesion is completely captured.

Stage 5: Deploy Clip

This stage is where after obtaining confirmation that the lesion is completely captured, the endoscopic clip is deployed to trap the lesion. We will use the OVESCO over-the-scope clip (OTSC).

We selected OTSC because OTSC achieved the highest closure rate of 98.2% (Dohi et al., 2020). The advantages of OTSC over TTSC are: (1) larger lesions can be closed by one clip (limited by cap diameter and flexibility of the tissue being pulled into the cap); and (2) greater compression force. Also, the nitinol clip provides constant compression forces of 8 to 9 Newton, which is necessary for successful permanent closure of defects (Mennigen et al., 2014; Kirschniak et al., 2007).

Stage 6: Slice Base

This stage is where the snare is heated and then pulled, slicing the base of cylindrical section. The subepithelial lesion is resected above the endoscopic clip. The mucosa and submucosal defects are closed by the previously applied endoscopic clip.

11. Future Work

Our projected timeline for future work is shown in Figure 18. We have identified the problem and the needs of the clinicians, nurses and patients at the Ambulatory Endoscopy Centre (AEC) at SGH. We have been working closely with our clinical mentor, Dr. Ravishankar Asokkumar and our industry mentor, Mr. Peter Cheng in our concept development. Following which, we have also developed our first prototype and will proceed on to further testing. Moving forward, we will work with our mentors to test our prototype further and conduct preliminary data collection.

We hope to build a complete, physical device showcasing the functionality of our full thickness resection device and collect data using a stomach analogue to demonstrate that it is just as safe and effective as surgical wedge resection, while being faster than ESD. We also hope to show that our device can securely capture the subepithelial lesion and ensure complete resection while reducing the risk of perforation and retaining resection efficiency and validate that our device has indeed improved on challenges faced by endoscopists when using the FTRD® System.

First, we need to design a new clip with a new clip release mechanism. Dr. Ravi does not like the OTSC clip and wants a circular closing clip which can clip and cut consecutively in a single mechanism immediately after determining enough tissue has been pulled in, because the stomach is constantly moving.

Second, we need to figure out the cookie cutter deployment mechanism. We are considering using a pneumatic or hydraulic system, using either air or saline to deploy the cookie cutter to secure the lesion boundary.

Third, we need to ensure that the cap can form a vacuum seal around the lesion. We intend to prototype the suction force using a syringe to generate the vacuum.

Fourth, we need to validate the effectiveness of the miniature ultrasound probe to obtain accurate visual feedback that the lesion is fully captured under the mucosal surface.

Last, we need to make a better stomach analogue using silicone or a pig stomach to model the multiple layers (mucosa, submucosa, muscularis propria and serosa), the texture and flexibility of the stomach wall. This will allow us to collect sufficient data to validate the effectiveness of our procedure and device.
